Airline Customer Satisfaction Analysis

Problem

An airline holds 130,000 customer surveys but no clear answer on which service upgrades would actually raise satisfaction.

Solution

Cleaned and explored the data with Python, then trained logistic regression and decision tree models to predict satisfaction and rank what drives it.

Result

78.6% prediction accuracy — tuned to 85% precision — and a clear investment priority: inflight entertainment, seat comfort, then online booking.

expand_moreStep-by-step write-up

The Dataset

Nearly 130,000 airline passenger surveys covering who the customers are, what kind of flight they took, and how they rated 23 aspects of the experience. The goal: figure out what actually makes a customer satisfied — and build a model that can predict it.

Step 1 — Getting the Data Ready

  • Loaded the raw survey data with Python and reviewed its structure to understand what each column held and where problems might hide.
  • Found gaps in the arrival-delay records and filled them with sensible middle-of-the-road values so no surveys had to be thrown away.
  • Confirmed customers split roughly 50/50 between satisfied and dissatisfied — a healthy balance for fair model training.

Step 2 — Exploring the Data

  • Charted every feature to build a picture of the customer base: mostly returning customers, mostly business travel, with seat comfort and food rated decently but not exceptionally.
  • Compared groups against satisfaction: male passengers were more likely to be dissatisfied, and business travelers were happier than people flying for personal reasons.
  • Measured how ratings move together and found a strong link between easy online booking and a smooth boarding experience — customers who struggled with one usually struggled with the other.

Step 3 — Preparing for Prediction

  • Translated text answers (gender, customer type, travel class) into a numeric format a model can learn from.
  • Set aside a portion of the data the model never sees during training, so its accuracy could be tested honestly on "new" customers.
  • Built reusable scoring tools to grade every model the same way and show exactly where predictions were right or wrong.

Step 4 — First Model: Logistic Regression

  • The first model predicted satisfaction correctly 78.6% of the time, and performed just as well on customers it had never seen — a sign it learned real patterns, not coincidences.
  • Dug into its mistakes from a business angle: the costliest error is marking an unhappy customer as happy, because those customers quietly leave and their issues never get fixed.
  • Adjusted how confident the model must be before calling someone "satisfied," cutting those costly misses — 85% of its "satisfied" calls were now right.

Step 5 — Second Model: Decision Tree

  • Built a decision-tree model — one that works like a flowchart of yes/no questions — and systematically tested different settings to find its best-performing version.
  • The tree revealed what drives satisfaction most, in order: inflight entertainment first, then seat comfort, then ease of online booking.

Step 6 — What the Airline Should Do

  • Recommended the decision tree as the final model — accurate, and easy for non-technical stakeholders to read and act on.
  • Advised investing in inflight entertainment and seat comfort first, creating premium bundles for short-haul business flyers, and adding real-time feedback channels to catch unhappy customers early.
